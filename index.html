From Radar to AI

A guide to the technology revolution, for the generation that watched it happen

1. We Are the Baby Boomers

Born after WWII to parents who survived the Depression and came home determined to build good lives. A country full of opportunity, an era of mechanical invention—belts, motors, on-off switches, bigger trucks and tractors. Your parents went from horse and buggy to the Moon in a single lifetime, and you inherited a world changing faster than anyone could keep up with. Nobody questioned how the TV worked. Nobody questioned the washing machine. The computer was the first invention that made people feel dumb for not understanding it, and that’s not fair.

2. “Just Don’t Turn Loose”

A kid born in 1998 who called himself Mean-Tough Cowboy Rogers and watched a VHS of Bodacious until the tape wore out. Grew up with computers the way Boomers grew up with TV—never questioned them, just used them. Got interested in technology, earned a full scholarship to UT Dallas, studied Computer Science and Cognitive Science, and became a software engineer at the FAA. One day, Grandma Rogers asked how a computer works, and the honest answer was: I don’t really know either. I just enjoy the fact that it does. That might be the whole thesis of this book.

3. From WWII Radar to the FAA

Grandma Rogers’ father trained in radar during WWII. Most people already know how to count the seconds between a flash of lightning and the thunder to figure out how far away the storm is. Radar works on the same principle—send out a signal, wait for the echo, and time the round trip. But instead of sound, radar uses radio waves, the same invisible waves that were already bringing families music and news in the living room. The WWII generation figured out how to time those echoes accurately enough to detect planes behind clouds, far beyond the horizon. The name says it all: RAdio Detection And Ranging. Most of what we do with radar today is built on what they figured out then.

4. Two Kids in a Garage

Computers already existed—some as big as a whole room. But you had to basically be an engineer to use one. Bill Gates and Paul Allen were two teenagers in Seattle who figured out how to change that. They didn’t invent the computer. They made it so that a regular person could sit down at a desk and use one without knowing how it worked. That one idea changed everything, and it’s the reason you can hold more information in your phone right now than they had for the entire Moon landing.

5. What Is AI, Really?

At the most basic level, a computer stores information using tiny, microscopic components that are either charged or not charged—like millions of tiny batteries that are either full or empty. We just call them ones and zeros because it’s a convenient way to name two states. It’s a lot like Morse code: dots and dashes don’t mean anything on their own, but people agreed on patterns that let them send real messages. Computers do the same thing with ones and zeros. When Grandma Rogers’ friend at the library put all those books on the computer, she was basically translating them into a kind of Morse code the machine could store.

The brain works with the presence and absence of electrical signals too—neurons fire or they don’t. The idea of “neural networks” came from trying to make computers work a little more like that. It took decades before computers were small and fast enough to actually do it. That’s what ChatGPT and all the AI you hear about is based on—very fast pattern-finders, built loosely on how brains are wired.

6. Is Anybody Watching the Store?

A lot of Baby Boomers worry that AI could get out of control. A lot of people who work in technology share that concern. AI safety is a real field, with real people working on it full-time, and it’s part of why I started studying it. The instinct to ask whether anybody is paying attention—that instinct is right. It’s not a dumb question. It might be the most important question there is.

7. What’s Coming Next

Self-driving cars. Brain-computer interfaces that might help stroke victims speak again. GPS that knows more than the astronauts did. Technology is moving fast, and some of it is exciting, and some of it is worth being cautious about. The generation that watched all of this happen has earned the right to ask hard questions about where it’s going.
